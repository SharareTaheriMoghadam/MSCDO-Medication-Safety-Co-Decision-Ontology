# MSCDO: Medication Safety Co-Decision Ontology

## Overview

MSCDO (Medication Safety Co-Decision Ontology) is an OWL 2 ontology designed to provide a unified semantic framework for medication safety, explainable clinical decision support, and human–AI collaborative decision-making.

The ontology integrates medication knowledge, patient-specific clinical context, medication-related risks, explainable recommendations, clinician oversight mechanisms, and patient safety concepts into a single interoperable knowledge model. It supports semantic interoperability, ontology-driven reasoning, and the development of trustworthy AI systems for healthcare.

The ontology has been implemented in Protégé, evaluated using Description Logic reasoning, SWRL rule execution, semantic querying, and scenario-based inference testing, and is publicly available through NCBO BioPortal. 2

---

# Research Motivation

Medication-related harm remains one of the leading preventable causes of patient injury worldwide.

Although numerous biomedical ontologies and clinical terminologies exist, they typically address isolated aspects of medication safety such as drug representation, adverse events, pharmacovigilance, or patient context.

MSCDO unifies these complementary perspectives into a single ontology that supports:

- Explainable Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS)
- Human–AI collaborative decision-making
- Semantic interoperability
- Knowledge graph construction
- Ontology-driven reasoning
- Medication safety intelligence

---

# Core Conceptual Layers

MSCDO is organized into six interconnected semantic layers:

- Medication Entity
- Patient Context
- Medication-Related Risk and Safety
- Decision and Explainability
- Human–AI Interaction
- Patient Safety and Prevention

Together these layers provide a comprehensive semantic representation of medication knowledge, patient characteristics, medication hazards, explainable recommendations, clinician oversight, and preventive safety strategies. 3

# Semantic Reasoning

MSCDO supports ontology-driven reasoning through OWL axioms and executable SWRL rules.

Example inference scenarios include:

- High-risk medication combinations
- Pharmacogenomic dose adjustment
- Medication review triggering
- Human override review
- Emerging safety signal detection
- Enhanced patient monitoring

These rules demonstrate the ontology's capability to generate clinically meaningful and explainable inferences. 5
